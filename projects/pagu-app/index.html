Pagu app

UX Design, Research & Business

2025 - 2026

Scope: Germany & Brazil · Building a Collective App

Environment: B2C Service · FLINTA Community

In this project, I worked as a Co-founder, Lead Product Designer, UX Researcher, and Strategist, developing a platform and collective focused on creating safe and inclusive spaces for FLINTA individuals, strengthening real-life connections, fostering a sense of belonging, and encouraging participation through shared cultural and social experiences.

Process Overview

Three areas were developed:

  • Research

    Conducted research and benchmarks with apps like HER, MeetUp, Timeleft. Mapped user needs and pain points.

  • Business

    Created product overview, competitive analysis and business model.

  • Design

    Prototyped a first version of the app on Figma and designed the visual identity.

Research

To deeply understand the social behaviors, challenges, and desires of FLINTA individuals regarding connection, community, and event participation.

Participants

  • 260+ responses
  • Diverse backgrounds: respondents from Europe, South America, North America, and beyond
  • Ages 18-45+
  • All identified within the FLINTA spectrum

Methodology

  • Online survey distributed via community channels, queer collectives, and social media
  • Mix of quantitative (multiple choice, scales) and qualitative (open-ended) questions

Themes explored

  • Community & connection needs
  • Event experiences (positive & negative)
  • Safety & comfort zones
  • Preferred communication & platforms
  • Barriers to participation in public spaces

Main Insights

Need for safe, curated spaces

  • 82% feel unsafe or excluded in mainstream social or cultural events
  • Strong desire for safe, FLINTA-centered environments

Desire for genuine social bonds

  • Over 70% want deep, platonic or interest-based connections, not just dating
  • Craving for spaces where shared values and identities are the foundation

Event fatigue & accessibility issues

  • Common struggles: overstimulation, social anxiety, lack of info on inclusive events
  • Clear preference for small, intentional gatherings with clear accessibility guidelines

Digital disconnection

  • Many feel digital platforms are not designed for FLINTA needs
  • People want tools that are community-first, not exploitative or algorithmic

User journey & features

  • Profile creation focused on personal and community safety
  • Event discovery with geolocation and category preferences
  • Real-time matching and recommendations based on behavior and feedback
  • AI-driven algorithm with personalized experiences

Differentiators

  • Enhanced safety features
  • Human verification to provide inclusivity of all people across the FLINTA spectrum

Design

What the brand ultimately represents

Pagu is more than a logo or an app.

It's a safe cultural space where FLINTA people can:

  • Meet without fear
  • Connect beyond romance
  • Be visible without performing
  • Build together without hierarchy

The branding exists to support that mission - quietly, confidently, and with care.

The Logo

Circle + Star (Iris)

The core symbol of Pagu is a circle with a star, inspired by the iris of an eye.

The iris represents:

Presence and attention
Humanity, intimacy, and closeness
Being seen and seeing others
Care and awareness

The choice is intentional:

Pagu exists to look at people as they are, not as profiles, categories, or data points. The symbol reflects a space where individuals are recognized, not optmized.

The star inside the circle adds:

Direction and guidance
Possibility and orientation
Individual brilliance within a collective

Together, the shape communicates soft strenght: protective but open, simple but symbolic, intimate without being exclusive.

What I Learned

  • Building for marginalized communities demands a design approach that center emotional safety, participatory research, and transparency at every step
  • Research with underrepresented groups must be approached with care, trust-building, and a non-extractive mindset
  • Solutions for connection go beyond features - they must reflect belonging, autonomy, and respect
  • Community-first design challenges traditional platform models that prioritize engagement over well-being
